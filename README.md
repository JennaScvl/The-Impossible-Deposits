# The Impossible Artifacts

*A history in documents, 1934–2134*

---

## PART ONE: THE FIND

---

*From the field diary of Dr. Evelyn Marsh, Director, Fitzwilliam–Cairo Expedition, Western Valley of the Kings*

*28 November 1934.*

The doorway is intact.

I write those words and my hand is not entirely steady. Twelve years since Carter and Carnarvon, and every season since, the Valley has been declared exhausted by men who did not care to dig in the western arm because the western arm is a long walk in bad light. We dug in the western arm.

Necropolis seals in the plaster, unbroken. The jackal and nine captives, and below it a cartouche none of us can place, Bishara reads it *Neferkare Setepenre*, a throne name unattested in any king list. An ephemeral king of the dynasty's chaotic end, reigning perhaps months, buried in haste and forgotten so thoroughly that even the tomb robbers, who forgot no one, forgot him.

The rubble fill behind the door is cemented with flood silt. Whatever is below has not been entered since that silt came down the wadi, and Bishara says the flood debris in this stretch of the Valley has lain undisturbed since before the Third Intermediate Period. Wilkes has photographed the sealing from nine angles. Tomorrow we breach.

*1 December 1934.*

Sixteen steps, a descending corridor, a second sealed doorway. Antechamber modest by Tutankhamun's standard, this was a poor king, or a rushed one, but complete. Chariot fittings, boxes of provisions, dried garlands, dockets in ink. The air was old. There is a smell an unopened tomb has that I will not attempt for this page.

*4 December 1934.*

The burial chamber.

I must set this down carefully, because I am aware that this page may one day be evidence, though of what I cannot say.

The sarcophagus lid had been displaced in antiquity, not by robbers, but by the burial party itself, it appears, who lacked the tackle to seat it true and shimmed it with limestone spall. Through the gap, by torchlight, we could see the coffin, and the coffin was open. No, not open. Never closed. The mummy lies wrapped and garlanded in an open anthropoid coffin of painted wood, and the funeral goods are heaped close about it, and everything speaks of a burial conducted at a run.

Resting upon the chest of the king, square upon the crossed arms, placed with what I can only call reverence, is a carton.

I mean the word precisely. A carton. Printed paperboard, perhaps ten inches by four, in colours, red, gold, a lurid cream-yellow, that do not belong to any palette I know from the second millennium before Christ, because they do not belong to the second millennium before Christ. There is writing on it. The writing is in English.

The writing says TWINKIES.

Wilkes made his exposures in absolute silence. Bishara stood at my shoulder and did not move for some minutes, and then said, quietly, in French, which he retreats to when upset: *"Madame, someone has been in this tomb."*

And I said: "Girgis, you sealed the corridor photographs yourself. Someone has *not* been in this tomb."

We looked at each other for a long time.

We have touched nothing. Wilkes will photograph everything twice, on separate plates, and I have posted Ahmed and his brother at the tomb mouth with instructions that no one, I told them: *no one, effendi or not*, descends without myself and Mr. Bishara together.

I do not know what I have found. I know what it looks like I have found, which is the end of my career, my expedition, and possibly the credibility of Egyptian field archaeology for a generation.

*Letter, Dr. Evelyn Marsh to Prof. Lionel Grieve, Trinity College, Cambridge, 19 December 1934 (marked PERSONAL, BURN, *not burned*)*

Lionel,

You will have seen the Times. "INTACT ROYAL TOMB IN THE VALLEY OF KINGS, UNKNOWN PHARAOH." All of it true. The tomb is real, the king is real, the finds are magnificent, and the Antiquities Service is delighted with us.

What the Times has not seen is Object 113.

I enclose one print. Look at it before you read on.

Yes. It is what it appears to be. It is a box of Twinkies. I am told, I have made discreet enquiries through Hartley's cousin in Chicago, that "Twinkies" are a sponge-cake confection introduced by the Continental Baking Company in 1930, sold two to a packet in waxed paper for a nickel. Four years old, Lionel. The product is four years old.

Except that the object on the king's chest is not that product. Hartley's cousin sent me a genuine packet by post. It is a flimsy thing in waxed paper with a paper band. Object 113 is a printed *carton*, claiming on its face to hold ten cakes "individually wrapped," in a slick material that is not cellophane and not waxed paper and which none of us can identify. The printing is finer than anything I have seen on any package anywhere, finer than the printing in *books*. There are blocks of dense small text in a tabulated format listing quantities of things called "sodium" and "polysorbate." There is a rectangle of narrow black stripes, and a smaller square of black-and-white chequerwork like a tiny abstract mosaic, and strings of letters and numerals that mean nothing: a code of some kind.

So: a hoax. Obviously a hoax. A hoax requires a hoaxer with access, and here is my difficulty, and I want you to attend to it with that merciless mind of yours, because I have been three weeks trying to break my own evidence and I cannot.

The corridor fill was cemented with ancient flood silt, photographed in section as we cut it. The necropolis sealings were intact and are themselves now under seal. The dust, Lionel, the *dust*. There was better than a centimetre of fine grey dust upon every surface in that chamber, laid down in visible fine layers like a pastry, and it lay *upon and around Object 113* in perfect continuity with the dust on the mummy, the coffin, the floor. There were dried insect fragments in it. A hoaxer must have entered a sealed and flood-cemented tomb without disturbing it, deposited the object, and then replaced a centimetre of layered dust, undisturbed, over the whole chamber including his own footprints.

Alternatively the hoaxer is on my staff and did it during the four days between breach and burial chamber. Wilkes's plates rule it out. The object is visible, I have checked with a glass, and you may check the enclosed print, in the very first exposure made through the gap in the sarcophagus lid, before any person had entered the chamber, in dust.

I have considered whether the hoaxer is myself and I am mad. I have considered whether it is Bishara. Bishara has considered whether it is me. We have concluded that we trust each other slightly more than we trust the alternative, which is nothing, because there is no alternative.

Here is what we have done, and I want your blessing or your veto by return post.

We have suppressed it. Object 113 does not appear in the register; the numbers run 112, 114, and if anyone asks, 113 was a duplicate entry struck out. The carton is crated, sealed, and will travel to Cairo as "ostraca, misc." Wilkes's duplicate plates and a set of dated prints have been lodged, sealed, with Barclays in Cairo, with a notarised statement by the three of us.

We cannot announce it: we should be laughed out of the profession, and worse, the *tomb* would be called a fraud, and every genuine object in it poisoned by association. We cannot explain it. We cannot bring ourselves to destroy it, because we are, God help us, archaeologists.

Therefore we shall study it. Quietly. For as long as it takes to understand what was done to us, and by whom, and how.

I expect it will take a year or two.

Yours in some distress,
E.M.

*Memorandum of understanding, handwritten, three signatures, Cairo, 4 January 1935*

Known informally thereafter as *the Concordat*.

Object WV27-113 ("the Confection") shall be preserved unaltered save for sampling as agreed by all custodians. Knowledge of the Object shall be confined to persons approved unanimously by the custodians, each of whom shall before death or retirement name and instruct a successor. No public statement shall be made until the Object's nature is understood. Every test that can be brought against the Object's authenticity *shall* be brought against it, as methods allow, in perpetuity. All results shall be recorded, however embarrassing.

*E. Marsh. G. Bishara. T. C. Wilkes.*

Beneath, in Marsh's hand, added later in different ink:

*Clause 3 was written in the belief that understanding was a matter of months. (E.M., 1949)*

*Comparison memorandum, Prof. L. Grieve, Cambridge, March 1935*

Subject: materials of Object 113 versus commercial reference specimens (Continental Baking Co. product, purchased Chicago, Feb. 1935; sundry British and American packagings).

The board first. The carton stock is a machine-made paperboard of remarkable uniformity, clay-coated on the print face. Fibre furnish is chemical wood pulp. Nothing impossible here, only implausibly *good*: calliper variation across the blank is under a thousandth of an inch. No mill I have consulted (discreetly, as "a question from a client in lithography") holds such tolerances in production.

The print. This is the sticking point. The image is composed of minute regular dots, as in half-tone work, but in four transparent inks laid in perfect register at a fineness I estimate at three to four times the best rotogravure of my acquaintance, and by a process that has left *no impression* in the board whatever. The ink film sits on the coating as if breathed there. I do not know of this process. I have shown a cropped fragment (lettering excluded) to two trade printers. Both asked me who had done it and one offered to buy the plates.

The inner wrappers, visible through the carton window: a thin, glossy, flexible film, isotropic in appearance, heat-sealed, *heat-sealed*, note, not gummed. It is not cellophane; it does not behave like any cellulose derivative I possess. Density and burning behaviour suggest a paraffinic synthetic. I am aware of laboratory curiosities in polymerised olefines. I am not aware of anyone wrapping *cakes* in them.

My conclusion. If this is a forgery, it was not made to deceive an Egyptologist. Every element of it is wrong for antiquity and half the elements are wrong for *the present*. It is as if the forger had set out to imitate not the past but a rather breathless future, and had then hidden his masterpiece where its only effect would be to make three respectable scholars doubt their sanity.

I confess the problem is growing on me. You may put me down as a custodian.

L.G.

## PART TWO: THE LONG CUSTODY

---

*Letter, G. Bishara to E. Marsh, Luxor, September 1943*

Evelyn,

The crate went south with the museum evacuation as arranged, listed as ostraca. I rode with it the whole way and slept on it two nights, which I mention only so that you may enjoy the picture: the Keeper of an impossible object using it as a pillow while the world burns.

You ask whether, with the war on, any of it matters. I have thought about this. I believe it matters more. If we are all to be judged by what we preserved, I intend to preserve one perfectly documented impossibility, in case it should someday be the most important thing anyone preserved at all.

Also, frankly, I want to know. Ten years now, and I still want to know. I suspect that is the true engine of the Concordat and everything else is stationery.

Your friend,
Girgis

*From the private diary of Prof. L. Grieve, Cambridge, 14 June 1952*

Dined at high table next to a visiting American chemist, very entertaining on the subject of his country's food. He held forth on a certain snack cake, *our* snack cake, though of course he could not know, and declared, to laughter, that the things were built to survive anything, that they never staled, that a packet of them would outlast the pyramids.

The pyramids.

I looked into my glass for a moment. In our vault in Cairo there is a specimen that has spent three thousand years in conditions any pyramid would envy, and I have seen the sampling reports on the contents, and I said nothing.

I have become very good at saying nothing.

*Internal report, Custodial file 113/22, "Carbon-14 assay, blind submission", November 1956*

Through an intermediary, three samples were submitted to a commercial-academic radiocarbon laboratory as unprovenanced material from "a private collection":

Sample A was linen from the king's outer wrappings. Sample B was a fragment of the cake residue (the contents are entirely desiccated; the cream centre survives as a friable brown lamina, on which subject the custodians have agreed to make no further remarks in writing). Sample C was carton board fibre, 40 mg, taken from the concealed base flap.

Results: Sample A: 3,240 ± 190 years before present. Consistent with the archaeology. Good. Sample B: 910 ± 160 years before present. Sample C: 1,080 ± 170 years before present.

Note appended by Prof. Grieve:

The linen behaves. The Confection does not... but observe *how* it does not. A modern hoax should assay as modern. An ancient object should assay as ancient. Object 113 assays as *mediaeval*, which satisfies nobody and was on no one's list of hypotheses.

Dr. Whitfield-Sayce proposes the obvious rescue: contamination with "dead" carbon, mineral oil, coal-tar derivatives, some petroleum ingredient in the cake or the board diluting the radiocarbon and inflating the age. It is a perfectly good explanation. I dislike it for one reason only, which I record so that a future custodian with better instruments may check it: the *dilution is too consistent*. Cake, board, and (by the flotation test) the wrapper film all sit in the same anomalous band, as though everything organic in the object had been grown under an atmosphere systematically poorer in radiocarbon than any atmosphere on record.

I can think of no natural way to arrange that. I note for completeness that I can think of one unnatural way, and that I have crossed it out. ~~If the atmosphere itself were to become sufficiently~~

File and continue.

*From the diary of Dr. Naguib Rashid, Keeper, Custodial file, Cairo, 30 June 1974*

Item in this week's *Time* magazine, which I take for the American packaging advertisements (my colleagues believe this is an eccentricity; it is the most serious thing I do): in an Ohio supermarket this month, a packet of chewing gum was passed over an electronic reader, which read a printed symbol and rang up the price automatically.

The symbol is a rectangle of narrow black stripes.

I went down to Room 9 tonight with the magazine and a hand lens and stood a long time.

Forty years the stripes have been in the file as "decorative element, function unknown." Marsh went to her grave calling them "the little fence." They are not a fence. They are machine writing, and the machine has only now been built, and the world has only now caught up to the *label on the box*, never mind the box.

I have written to the other custodians proposing a new working assumption, and I will record it here in plain words since the file is sealed regardless: the Object is not imitating anything. It is an ordinary industrial product of a period that has not yet occurred, and our discipline's entire toolkit is designed for the opposite problem.

Archaeology dates things that are older than they look. God help us, we have found something younger than its stratigraphy.

There remains the chequered square, which no reader in Ohio or anywhere else can yet explain. I find I am no longer impatient. The stripes took forty years. I can wait for the mosaic.

*Report, Custodial file 113/47, "Wrapper polymer, differential scanning calorimetry and infrared assay", University contact (unattributed), 1979*

The film is biaxially oriented polypropylene, metallised on the inner face, with a heat-seal coating and an antioxidant package. Every element of this is, as of this decade, commercially current, the anomaly of 1935 has quietly evaporated; Grieve's "paraffinic synthetic" is now on every sweet counter in Europe.

But the film is also *old*. Oxidative chain scission, additive migration and bloom, seal-layer embrittlement: the degradation chemistry is consistent, under Room 9 conditions plus three millennia of dry sealed-tomb storage, with an age on the order of thousands of years, and is wildly inconsistent with an age of decades. We attempted to reproduce the degradation profile by accelerated aging of modern reference film. Our best forced specimens look, under the microscope, like a man of thirty in stage paint. The Object's wrapper looks like an old man, because the only way to look like an old man is the slow way.

Summary position, 45 years into the Concordat: manufacture indicates the future; every physical clock in the object indicates the ancient past; and the two families of evidence refuse, with increasing rudeness, to compromise.

Addendum, handwritten (Rashid): At the faculty party for this report's unwitting author, a guest joked that after the atomic war ends the world, the survivors will be the cockroaches, and these cakes, which as everyone knows last forever.

Everyone laughed. I laughed.

Well, I thought. About that.

*Custodial file 113/63, Micro-stratigraphy of chamber dust (samples archived 1934, re-sectioned 1988–1991)*

Marsh's insistence in 1934 on lifting intact dust columns in gelatine was regarded by Wilkes as theatrical. It has proved to be the single most valuable decision in the file.

The dust overlying and adjacent to Object 113 preserves thirty-one distinguishable laminae, correlating bed-for-bed with control columns from the far side of the chamber. Within them: there is a silt-enriched band, geochemically matched to flood deposits in neighbouring tombs and dated by association to a storm event in approximately the 9th century BCE, present *above* the Object, undisturbed. There is limestone spall micro-debris from at least two seismic episodes, likewise above. There are pollen spectra consistent throughout with local flora, including taxa that declined regionally after the Ptolemaic period, in the upper laminae only, exactly as in the controls. There are fragmentary remains of dermestid beetles in three laminae, including a form not recorded in the Theban necropolis in modern collections.

There is no discontinuity. There is no infill, no resettled dust, no lamina that occurs beside the Object but not upon it. Whoever placed the carton in that chamber did so before the ninth century BCE, or else replaced three thousand years of dust, flood silt, earthquake spall, pollen rain, and dead beetles, in order, without error, in the dark.

The custodians note, per Clause 5, that we have now spent several meetings seriously debating the second option.

---

*Custodial file 113/71, Note on labelling regulation, 1994; extended 2016; extended 2043*

1994: The United States mandates a standardised "Nutrition Facts" panel on packaged foods. Its format, rules, weights, ordering, matches the tabulated block on Object 113 in general architecture, though not in detail. The Object's panel contains lines with no regulatory meaning: among them "Added Sugars," and below the table two small certification marks and an indexed figure labelled in a phrasing that corresponds to nothing in any jurisdiction.

2016: "Added Sugars" becomes a mandatory line item. One discrepancy retired.

2043: The Pan-American food-labelling accord introduces a mandatory processed-food index, displayed as a small boxed figure beneath the nutrition panel. Format, placement, and typography match the Object. Another discrepancy retired.

The Keeper wishes the following observation entered formally, because it has become the file's quiet terror: *the Object is a self-authenticating document on a timer.* We have stopped asking whether the world will finish converging on it. We now ask only *when*, and we note that the remaining unmatched features, the chequered code, the lot string, the trade dress itself, are precisely those tied not to law or technology but to one company's internal decisions.

The Concordat was written to last until we understood the Object. It appears instead that it will last until the Object is *in shops*.

---

*Transcript fragment, oral history recording, sealed, Dr. Ilse Nakamura, Keeper (11th), recorded 2059*

...people will ask, when this opens, whenever it opens, people will ask: a hundred and twenty-five years, how could you sit on it? And I want whoever is listening to understand that we did not sit. There has never been an artefact in the history of the discipline tested like this one. Every generation of custodians made the same wager: *our* new methods will finally catch the forger. Radiocarbon. Chromatography. Electron microscopy. Racemisation clocks in the filling proteins, yes, we dated the *cream*, it dates ancient, next question. Thermoluminescence of the adhering dust. Accelerator mass spec, at three labs, blind. In the thirties we sampled cosmogenic isotopes in the board, carbon-14 made *in situ* by muons, in an object sitting under thirty metres of limestone; the accumulation profile says it sat under that limestone for roughly three thousand years, and there is no way to fake muon exposure, you cannot bribe muons...

[Interviewer: And the radiocarbon anomaly?]

That's the one that keeps me up. Grieve's "mediaeval cake," 1956, everyone's favourite contamination story for eighty years. You know what it is now? You can see it in his file, he *saw* it, he crossed it out. The carbon in that cake was grown under an atmosphere diluted with fossil carbon far beyond 1930s levels. Beyond ours, for most of my career. We have watched the atmospheric curves climb toward the Object's values my whole tenure. The current projections put the crossover in the early 2060s. The wheat in that cake has not been *grown* yet, and I can tell you the approximate year it will be grown, from isotopes, out of a sealed tomb. That is not archaeology. I don't know what that is. There is no name for the thing that is.

[Interviewer: Do you believe you'll see the end of it?]

I believe the end of it is in a warehouse somewhere being designed by a brand team who have no idea, and I believe it will not announce itself to a laboratory. It'll be a Tuesday. It'll be somebody's errand. That's the shape of this whole thing, you understand, it has never once had the decency to be dramatic.

## PART THREE: CONVERGENCE

---

*Columbus, Ohio, 14 March 2061*

It was a Tuesday. It was an errand.

Dr. Ilse Nakamura, emerita, eighty-one years old and eleventh Keeper of a file she had long since concluded she would hand on unresolved, was buying coffee, lentils, and cat food. She was not thinking about Room 9. One did not survive four decades of custodianship by thinking about Room 9 in supermarkets.

She turned down the snack aisle because it was a shortcut to the cat food.

She stopped.

Later, in the deposition, she was asked how long she stood there, and answered that she didn't know, but that an employee had asked her twice if she was all right, and that she had said, "Yes, thank you, I've just seen a box."

Red. Gold. The lurid cream-yellow. The window. TEN CAKES, INDIVIDUALLY WRAPPED. New trade dress, said a small violet flash on the shelf edge. NEW LOOK, SAME CLASSIC TWINKIES.

She took out her reading glasses. She looked at the nutrition panel, at the boxed index figure beneath it, at the small chequered square, at the lot string printed along the base flap in the format she had known by heart since she was thirty-four:

L4 · 073 · EMP

Line four. Day seventy-three. Emporia.

Dr. Nakamura checked the date on her phone. Day seventy-three of 2061 was that day, that Tuesday, the fourteenth of March. Somewhere in Kansas, at that hour, the line was running.

She stood in the aisle of a grocery store and looked at an ordinary object on an ordinary shelf, of which there existed exactly one other example in the world, currently 3,395 years old.

Then she bought eleven of them, which emptied the shelf, and drove home very carefully, the way one does when transporting the resolution of a century.

*Custodial file 113/88, Forensic comparison, Object 113 vs. production batch L4-073-2061-EMP (conducted with manufacturer's cooperation under seal), August 2061*

Under a non-disclosure instrument whose negotiation the file politely declines to describe, the brand's current corporate parent, its fifth, granted access to line records, print tooling, and materials certifications.

The findings: the trade dress is identical to the 2061 refresh in every element, to the limit of measurement. The design was developed 2058–2060 by an external agency; the design files, versioned and timestamped, show ordinary iteration, seventeen rejected comps, one crisis over the yellow, with no discontinuity, no incursion, and no personnel with any conceivable link to the file. The lot string L4 · 073 · EMP conforms to the plant coding scheme introduced company-wide in January 2061. Line 4, Emporia, ran the new carton on day 073. Object 113 is, by its own label, a unit of that batch. The print: the rotary process leaves a periodic micro-defect from a scratch on cylinder 3 (magenta), present across the sampled 2061 run. The identical defect, at identical period and phase, is present on Object 113, overlaid, in the Object's case, by thirty-three centuries of ink-film degradation. Board, film, adhesives, resin batch markers: matched, matched, matched, matched.

The two objects differ in exactly one measurable respect: age.

Conclusion for the custodians (Clause 5, plain words, as tradition now requires): Object 113 is a genuine, ordinary carton from the production run of 14 March 2061, and it was excavated, sealed in undisturbed New Kingdom stratigraphy, on 4 December 1934, one hundred and twenty-six years, three corporate bankruptcies, and one hundred and eleven analytical campaigns before it was manufactured.

We have completed the task set for us in 1935. We have not understood the Object. We have, however, run out of ways to be wrong about it, which the Keeper submits is the same thing wearing laboratory clothes.

Recommend disclosure.

*The Cairo Disclosure, press conference, Egyptian Museum, 9 June 2062 (transcript excerpt)*

DR. NAKAMURA: ...and so the deposition lodged with Barclays in 1935 was opened yesterday before notaries, representatives of the Ministry, and three independent forensic photography teams. The plates are genuine, unaltered, and were sealed in January 1935. You have the enlargements in your materials. The barcode on the carton in those 1934 photographs scans. It returns the correct product. The QR code resolves. It resolves to a promotional address registered by the manufacturer in 2060, which currently offers a coupon.

[Prolonged disturbance.]

Q: Dr. Nakamura, you're describing either the greatest fraud in scientific history or...

DR. NAKAMURA: Yes. Those have been the options since the fourth of December 1934, and I want to be precise with you about what a fraud requires here, because my predecessors spent their lives on this and I will not have it waved at. A fraud requires that persons unknown, in or before 1934, manufactured, using processes that would not exist for decades, a perfect unit of a carton that would not be designed for another century and a quarter, by an agency whose designers were not born; that they emplaced it in a flood-sealed tomb without disturbing thirty-one laminae of dust, silt, pollen, and dead beetles; that they arranged for its polymers, proteins, and in-situ cosmogenic isotopes to age three thousand years; and that they grew its wheat under an atmosphere that did not exist until my own old age. At which point, ladies and gentlemen, I put it to you that the word "fraud" is no longer doing any work in the sentence, and we should all have the honesty to sit quietly for a moment with what is.

Q: And what is left?

DR. NAKAMURA: What is left is a finding. The finding is this: the manufacturing chronology and the depositional chronology of Object 113 are both securely established, and they are incompatible with each other under any temporally local causal model. That is the finding. That is the entire finding. The Ministry will now take questions about the exhibition, which opens Thursday, and to answer the question every one of you flew here to ask: no. It will be displayed in its carton. The carton is the artefact. Nobody is going to eat it.

*Journal of Archaeological Science, vol. 214 (2062): reviewer comments on submission JAS-62-0331, "Decoupled Manufacturing and Depositional Chronologies in the WV27 Carton: A 126-Year Evidentiary Synthesis"*

REVIEWER 1: Methodologically unimpeachable, which is the problem. I have spent three weeks trying to find the flaw the authors missed. The authors missed nothing. Their predecessors missed nothing. I recommend publication and I resent it.

REVIEWER 2: The manuscript's conclusion, "no temporally local causal model accommodates the complete evidentiary record", is a masterpiece of the passive voice. Everyone understands what this sentence is for. It is a sentence-shaped object placed reverently over the thing nobody will write. I have tried, in the margin of my own copy, to draft the sentence the authors are avoiding, and I find I cannot make myself submit it in a signed review, which I suppose proves the authors' editorial judgment is sound. Accept.

REVIEWER 3 (declined to review, comment forwarded by editor): I was taught that extraordinary claims require extraordinary evidence. Nobody warned me the evidence might arrive first and simply wait.

## PART FOUR: NINETEEN DAYS

---

*Zaoyang, Hubei Province, 28 June 2062*

The Cairo Disclosure was nineteen days old, and Director Wen Zhaodi was on record about it.

She had been asked at a conference reception, and she had said, colleagues would remind her of the exact words for the rest of her life, that she was glad, at least, that Chinese archaeology dealt in bronzes, chariot pits, and waterlogged lacquer, and could be relied upon to remain boring. The waterlogged tombs of the old state of Chu were her speciality precisely because they were sealed systems: charcoal packing, metres of pounded white kaolin clay, groundwater, anoxia. Nothing entered. Nothing left. The chemistry was hard and the surprises were the good kind, silk, manuscripts, plums in a jar, once, famously, soup.

Tomb M17 at Zaoyang had been eleven days from chamber opening when Cairo happened.

On 28 June, with the clay dome sectioned, recorded, and breached, with the chamber flooded as expected and the borescope down among the drowned lacquerware, Director Wen stood at the monitor watching the feed with her site photographer, her conservators, and a student named Fan whose job was the log.

The borescope drifted past a food vessel, a nested set of cups, an object that would prove to be a zither.

Fan said, "Director, there's a..." and stopped logging.

At the bottom of the chamber, resting on its side against a bronze ding among the offering vessels, dulled by two millennia of anoxic water but entirely, structurally, unmistakably itself, was a beverage can.

The feed was silent for eight seconds. On the recording, which was played at conferences for the next fifty years, the eight seconds are the famous part.

Then Director Wen said, in the flat voice of a woman watching her own reception remarks travel back in time to find her, "Do not touch anything. Notarise the feed. All of it, from this morning. Get the ministry, get the notaries, get..." a pause, and then, with the exhaustion of the entire discipline arriving in her body at once, "get Cairo."

The can was upright in nothing, explained by nothing, red beneath the mineral film, and where the design curved around the body the English script was legible even on the borescope feed, because it is one of the half-dozen most recognisable marks ever made by human industry, and it says *Coca-Cola*.

Student Fan, resuming the log with the heroism of the very junior, entered: *One (1) container, metal, sealed, apparently unopened. Wave-form decorative band unfamiliar. Closure mechanism unfamiliar. Format: slim, approx. 250 ml.* And then, because the log was a legal document now and everyone knew where this was going, Fan added the line that became the discipline's shorthand for the entire second age of the problem:

*Manufacturing era: to be announced.*

*Joint communiqué, Hubei Provincial Institute of Cultural Relics and the Egyptian Ministry of Tourism and Antiquities, 30 August 2062*

...independent verification of the sealed context is complete. The kaolin clay dome above Tomb M17 was intact, continuously waterlogged, and geochemically undisturbed since closure in the fourth century BCE. Anoxic corrosion chemistry, mineral encrustation, sediment lamination, and isotopic assays of the vessel's contents (the can is full; the contents have not aged well; further remarks are declined) independently indicate continuous submersion for approximately 2,380 years.

The vessel's alloy, its closure, and elements of its trade dress correspond to no current or historical production of the manufacturer, which has confirmed the same in writing, in what its counsel termed "the company's strangest letter since 1985."

In view of the WV27 precedent, the following protocol ("the Wen Protocol") takes immediate effect for this and all future such finds:

There shall be full disclosure within ninety days. There will be no second Concordat; the first one is deemed to have used up the discipline's entire allotment. All analyses shall be pre-registered and blinded. Each participating institution shall lodge with notaries a sealed, dated prediction of the artefact's eventual manufacturing date, derivable from isotopic and materials trajectories.

The median sealed prediction, it can be disclosed, falls in the early twenty-second century.

The custodial tradition of plain words is hereby adopted as well: everyone now understands the shape of this. The object is real; the burial is real; the production run is pending. The community is advised to institute succession planning, as verification will be the work of colleagues not yet born, and to refrain from remarks at conference receptions.

## PART FIVE: THE DAY OF SEALING (INTERLUDE)

*Western Valley, in the reign of a king whose name would be forgotten by everyone except, eventually, everyone. Around 1334 BCE.*

---

The traveller had been in the Valley for six days, and the workers had decided he was harmless, which was the finest compliment the necropolis knew how to pay.

He had appeared on the path from the river with the correct passes, nobody could quite remember checking them, but nobody could remember *not* checking them either, dressed in good plain linen worn slightly wrong, the way linen is worn by a man who learned to wear it from a picture. He knew perhaps thirty words. He deployed them with immense care and total confidence, like a man laying out his entire fortune in small coins, and when the thirty words ran out he had his hands, his eyebrows, and food.

The food was the thing.

The burial was a hurried one, a brief king, a hot month, a tomb borrowed from an unfinished plan, and hurried burials run on bread and beer and short tempers. On the second day, at the rest hour, the traveller had sat down near the foreman Nakht's crew, opened a shoulder bag, and produced small golden loaves, each sealed in a skin like a soap bubble that crackled.

He opened one, ate half, and held the rest out to Nakht with the universal expression that says: *it would honor me, and also I am not poisoning you, see, I ate some.*

Nakht, who as foreman held the crew's monopoly on necessary risks, ate it.

There was a silence of the kind that follows a man discovering a genuinely new experience quite late in life.

The cakes were soft in a way nothing was soft. They were sweet in a way that made honey seem like it had been holding back. Inside each one was a white cream for which the crew had no word, so they invented one, and the word did not survive, which the traveller would have agreed was probably for the best.

By the fourth day the crew had eaten a full box between them, the traveller pantomiming each time, with a modesty that seemed important to him, that there were more, that it was nothing, that where he came from these were *common*. This claim was regarded as the polite exaggeration of a rich foreigner. Nothing about the little loaves was common. The skins alone, transparent as still water and stronger than any membrane, were folded away carefully by the workers and taken home to wives who did not believe the explanations.

Where he came from was the subject of the crew's evening speculation. Very far, was the consensus. He had shown them, in gestures: a journey of the kind you measured with a raised arm swept slowly across the whole sky, many suns, many moons, hands opening and opening and opening, farther than Punt, farther than anywhere with a name. He had a way of looking at ordinary things, a chisel, a basket, the river light in the late afternoon, with an attention that was almost grief, as if he were memorizing them, or as if he had read about them all his life and could not believe he was permitted to stand among them.

He was, in short, a pilgrim. The Valley knew pilgrims. This one was simply better provisioned than most.

On the sixth day they sealed the king.

The chamber was crowded and hot, the lamps low on wicks and oil because the air was going bad, and the goods went in with the efficiency of men who respect the dead and are also paid by the job. Provisions were stacked by the coffin: bread in baskets, jars sealed with mud caps, a haunch, wine dockets inked in a fast hand.

The traveller stood at the edge of the lamplight where he had, by patient harmlessness, earned the right to stand, and watched the provisioning with visible approval. Food for the journey. This, his whole posture said, was a people who understood how to see someone off.

Then he stepped forward, and from his bag he brought out one more box. Unopened. The last one.

He held it in both hands, and there in the lamplight he performed, slowly, for Nakht and the lector priest and the crew, the finest work of the trip, the piece he had, in a sense, crossed an unimaginable distance to deliver. He pointed to the box. He pointed to himself, and swept his arm across the whole sky: *from very far.* He pointed to the coffin, to the wrapped king, and then raised his arm again and swept it across a different sky, slower, wider, the arc every man in the room recognized, because it was the arc of the sun through the Duat, the long dark voyage, the journey of the dead.

*He goes far too,* said the traveller's hands. *Farther than I came. He should have these for the road.*

And he held out the box toward the king, and bowed his head.

The lector priest looked at the foreman. The foreman looked at the priest. It was, when you thought about it, entirely proper. Provisioning the dead was the oldest courtesy there was; the strange loaves were, everyone could attest from direct and repeated personal experience, exceptionally good; and refusing a pilgrim's offering at a sealing invited exactly the kind of luck this rushed, understaffed, borrowed-tomb burial could not afford. The priest said a line over the box. The traveller stood back; it was important, his stillness said, that it not be his hands. It was Nakht's own hands that placed the offering with reverence on the chest of the king, square upon the crossed arms, where a king's provisions ought to be if the king should wake hungry in the dark.

It sat there, red and gold, catching the lamplight.

The traveller looked at it for a long moment with an expression the workers read as piety.

Then the lamps went out one by one toward the door, and the chamber was closed, and the corridor filled, and by dusk the plaster was going over the blocking under the necropolis seals, the jackal, the nine captives, the name of a king with no history, while the crew talked about dinner.

The traveller stood a little apart, watching the wet plaster take the seal impressions, and permitted himself, at last, the smile he had been rationing for six days.

He thought about laboratories. He thought about people not yet born in institutions not yet founded, in a century he had circled on no calendar, unpacking a crate. He thought about instruments that did not exist, built by careful, rigorous, decent people specifically to catch liars, applied one after another after another to the most honest object in the world. He thought about the dust, beginning tonight, settling one invisible grain at a time, every grain a witness on his behalf, three thousand years of testimony accumulating in the dark at no further effort on his part whatsoever.

He had not lied to anyone. That was the beautiful part. Every test would come back true, because everything about it was true, and the truth would be the whole problem.

"They're going to spend centuries trying to prove this is a hoax," he said quietly, in a language that would not exist for a very long time.

He paused. The last light was going off the cliffs, and somewhere down the path a worker was laughing at someone else's joke, and the seals were drying on the only door.

He grinned.

"They're going to be *so* annoyed."

Then he picked up his empty bag and walked away down the path from the Valley, toward the river, and was not seen again, then.

## PART SIX: THE HORIZONS

---

*Fundamentals of Archaeological Chronology, 9th edition (2131), Chapter 1, "Special Reference Points" (excerpt)*

The Twinkies Horizon (1334 ± 10 BCE deposition; 2061 CE manufacture) and the Coca-Cola Horizon (c. 320 BCE deposition; manufacture pending, projected 2140s–2160s CE) denote the two securely documented instances of manufacturing–deposition decoupling (MDD). The student should note the field's terminology with some care. "Horizon" is used in the standard archaeological sense, a chronological marker of exceptional reliability, and the usage is not ironic: no objects in the global record have better-constrained deposition dates, and one of them additionally possesses, uniquely among all artefacts ever excavated, a manufacture date documented to the day *by the manufacturer*.

The student should further note what the horizons are not. They are not evidence of anachronistic technology transfer: no site associated with either horizon shows any cultural, technical, or economic perturbation whatsoever. Fourth-century Chu did not learn to draw aluminium. The New Kingdom baked as it had always baked. The deposits changed nothing at their end of the interval, and at our end they have changed only us.

Finally, the student is cautioned regarding the standard conclusion formula, *"no temporally local causal model accommodates the evidentiary record"*, which will be encountered throughout the literature. The formula means precisely what the student, upon first reading, suspects it means. The student is advised that everyone knows this, that the formula is nonetheless the correct professional usage, and that the reasons for this are covered in Chapter 14, "Philosophy of the Discipline," and at any conference bar after 11 p.m.

*The Marsh–Bishara Bicentennial Lecture, Egyptian Museum, Cairo, 4 December 2134 (closing minutes)*

PROF. A. OKONKWO: ...two hundred years tonight, to the evening, since Evelyn Marsh sat in a tent in the Western Valley and wrote *I do not know what I have found.*

Let me tell you where her sentence stands after two centuries of the finest falsification campaign in the history of science. We know what she found. She found a carton of snack cakes from the fourteenth of March, 2061, resting on the chest of a king sealed away in 1334 BCE, and every clause of that sentence is now among the most exhaustively verified claims our species has ever produced. What we do not know, what two hundred years, eleven Keepers, the Wen Protocol, four hundred laboratories, and by conservative count eleven thousand published pages have not produced, is the sentence *after* that sentence. We possess, in two museum cases four thousand kilometres apart, a complete proof and no theorem. The evidence arrived first, and it is still waiting.

In Hubei, my colleagues enter year seventy-two of the vigil. The isotope trajectories have begun to converge; the sealed predictions of 2062 open on schedule; the manufacturer, which maintains, I quote the standing corporate communication, "no comment, ongoing cooperation, and considerable curiosity," reports nothing. Somewhere between next decade and my successor's successor's tenure, on some ordinary shelf, the second shoe drops. We have institutionalized patience. It is the discipline's strangest achievement: we are the only science with a *queue*.

And so to the question I am contractually asked at every one of these lectures, usually by a graduate student, and, yes, I see the hand, go ahead.

STUDENT: Professor, after all of it, who do you think they were?

PROF. OKONKWO: [pause] You know the joke in your cohort. Everyone in this room came up on it: that somewhere in the deep future, our species finally achieves the impossible, reaches back through time itself, the single greatest technological accomplishment in human history, and the individual entrusted with it, or who stole it, or who built it in a shed, surveys the entire expanse of the human past, every mystery, every library burning, every unanswerable question of who we are and how we came to be...

...and spends it on the longest, most meticulously documented practical joke ever played.

[Laughter.]

I used to tell my students that the joke was a way of not thinking about it. I've changed my mind, in my old age. I think the joke is the *finding*. Consider what was left for us. Not a weapon, not a warning, not a cure, not a text. Nothing that helped. Nothing that harmed. Two of the most ordinary objects industrial civilization ever produced, items whose entire cultural meaning is that they are cheap, and everywhere, and nothing, rendered priceless by placement alone. Whoever did this refused, with what I can only call discipline, to give us anything at all except *evidence*. They knew exactly what we would do with evidence, because it is the only thing they needed to know about us: that we would check. That we are the sort of species that, confronted with the impossible, forms a committee, and keeps the committee running for two hundred years, and files everything. They did not leave us a message. I have come to believe we, all of this, the file, the vigil, the eleven thousand pages, this lecture, *we* are the message, delivered by us, to ourselves, at their arrangement. It says: *you checked. Good.*

That, and, I am reliably informed by two centuries of documentation, they thought it would be funny.

[Pause.]

The infuriating thing, ladies and gentlemen, is that it is.

Thank you.

[Applause.]
